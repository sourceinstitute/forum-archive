Tobias Van Scheider - a fun-loving hipster designer at Spotify, talks about tool lust and embracing constraints. He argues that when you don‚Äôt have all the best, fancy tools, your work becomes better. And when you do have all the best, fancy tools, you need to create constraints.

This gets me thinking of different event formats we could use to get different things done, not just for us, but as open formats we can share with educators around the world.

The last five years I always participated in the Cannes Young Lions competition, an event by the Cannes Lions Festival. It works like this: On the date of the competition, you get a written briefing from a imaginary client telling you what problem to solve.

Then you have exactly 24 hours to do it. You are not allowed to ask any questions. 24 hours later you need to upload the deliverable. You are not even allowed to present your work, it has to speak for itself. (In my category ‚ÄúPrint Advertising‚Äù your deliverable is a one page ad)

There are many reasons why a competition like this is amazing:

  1. You only have 24h, get your shit together.

  2. I don‚Äôt even work in advertising. It‚Äôs a completely new challenge for me.

  3. Limited time means you have to focus on the idea. There is just no time for time consuming execution. That‚Äôs when you really learn that fancy Photoshop effects or 3D renderings are just distraction unless they ARE the idea. The same goes for writing beautiful code, no one cares.

  4. Learn to manage your time within just 24h. Don‚Äôt forget to eat and sleep. Not sleeping might lead to poor decisions in the final hour. But sleeping also means, you lose time. What do you chose?

  5. You have to balance between what idea to execute. Should you do something that is good for the client, or the big bang that the judges will love to see? These might be two different ideas.

Constraints breed creativity. They force you to act, to move, think sharp and find a solution.

Hackathons come to mind, but I challenge you to evolve that (now old) concept. What kind of maker/doer/solver event formats can we think of that build in a specific constraint to push a better kind of outcome?

This reminds me of a format I tried with @jansn back in the day.

Basically, it was a startup weekend but with mentors only. So the mentors would have their individual startup projects, and have peer-support of other mentor-calibre people with different skills.

Each had their own braintrust and the rule was you had to braintrust and pivot every 4 hours. (You‚Äôd call a personal 8-minute braintrust session on-demand.) Everyone was there as support for the others and could be pulled in. The pivoting needed some policing (refering to the time-keeper person) and each pivot was logged on a simple sticky-note dashboard.

It‚Äôs how I launched Signally.
